26 July 1934 — A life shaped by the sea

At sixteen years old, Norman Ahern signed on for sea school in Gloucester, collected his discharge book from the labour exchange on Lednal Street, and stepped aboard his first ship in the London Docks. This is his story.

The Early Years

How it all began

June 1951

Sea School, Gloucester

Norman signed up for an eight-week course at sea school in Gloucester — his first formal step into the Merchant Navy. He was sixteen years old.

Training

Late Summer 1951

Lednal Street, London

A week after finishing sea school, Norman reported to the Merchant Navy Pool office on Lednal Street in London. A clerk informed him of his allocation and assigned him to his first ship.

Assignment

Late Summer 1951

The Discharge Book

From the Labour Exchange, Norman was issued his Discharge Book — the official record carried by every Merchant Navy sailor. An entry of VG meant good conduct; DR would indicate the opposite. Norman's record would follow him across every ship and every ocean he sailed.

Official Record

Late Summer 1951

KG5 — First Ship, London Docks

Norman's first posting was to KG5 (King George V), moored in the London Docks. He walked aboard a merchant vessel for the very first time, and his seafaring life began.

The Voyage

Ports of call

Norman's first long voyage took him from the London Docks eastward through the Suez Canal and on across the Indian Ocean and South China Sea — a journey of thousands of miles that brought him face to face with a world still marked by the Second World War and stirring with new tensions.

London → Port Said → Suez Canal → Red Sea → Aden → Colombo → Bombay → Singapore → Penang → Hong Kong

Port Said

Egypt

En route

The gateway to the Suez Canal — Norman's first sight of Africa and the Arab world. Port Said was a busy, cosmopolitan port city at the northern entrance to the canal, a place where East and West collided in the dockside bazaars and coal depots. From here the ship moved south into the canal itself.

Suez Canal & Red Sea

Egypt / Arabia

Transit

The canal — barely wide enough in places for two ships to pass — cut through the desert for over a hundred miles. Beyond it lay the Red Sea, a narrow, blazing hot stretch of water flanked by the Arabian Peninsula and the Horn of Africa, leading south to Aden and the open Indian Ocean.

TransitRed Sea

Aden

Yemen

~4 nights

The ship stopped at Aden — then a British Crown Colony — for around four nights. Perched at the mouth of the Red Sea, it was one of the world's great refuelling and resupply ports, a rocky, sun-baked place with a deep natural harbour. For a seventeen-year-old from England, it was unlike anything he had seen before.

Colombo

Ceylon (Sri Lanka)

Stopover

The ship called at Colombo on the island of Ceylon — today's Sri Lanka — before continuing on to Bombay. A lush, tropical port city with a very different character to the arid ports of Arabia.

Indian OceanCeylon

Bombay

India

~1 week

Norman spent around a week in Bombay — India's great western port, teeming and enormous. The city had only recently gained independence from Britain in 1947 and was still finding its footing. The docks were vast, the streets extraordinary, and the scale of everything unlike anything Norman had encountered in England.

~1 weekPost-independence India

Singapore

Malaya

~1 week

Singapore in 1951 was on edge. Racial tensions between the Indian and Malay communities spilled into the streets, and Norman witnessed the unrest first hand. Despite this, the city's famous night markets were vivid and alive after dark — a sensory world of food stalls, traders, and noise that ran through the small hours.

One night, Norman and a friend strayed into the wrong part of the city. They were held at gunpoint — a man with a firearm told them in no uncertain terms they were not welcome in the area and ordered them to move on. They left quickly.

He also saw Raffles — the legendary colonial hotel and bar where the wealthy and the well-connected gathered, a world away from the dock streets Norman moved through.

Penang

Malaya · Island

Few nights

From Singapore, the ship sailed north-west to the island of Penang — a lush, green island off the Malayan coast. During his time ashore, Norman came across something that stopped him in his tracks: an old Japanese tank, still sitting in the street where it had been abandoned or destroyed at the end of the war just six years earlier. A raw reminder that the war had been fought right here, on these pavements, not some distant battlefield.

Few nights Japanese tank in the street Post-war Malaya

Hong Kong

British Colony

Extended stay

Hong Kong was where Norman spent the most time — working aboard ship, receiving cargo and customers, and exploring one of the most extraordinary cities on earth.

The Star Ferry. For just sixpence, Norman crossed the harbour from Kowloon on the mainland to Hong Kong Island — about a mile of open water. The ferry carried around 500 passengers at a time, packed with locals, traders, and the occasional merchant sailor.

The markets. Rolex watches — the real thing — were on sale at the market stalls for £25. The markets were dense, loud, and spilling with goods from across Asia.

Repulse Bay. Norman went swimming at Repulse Bay, the famous sheltered beach on the south side of Hong Kong Island — beautiful, and haunted by the violence of the Japanese invasion just a decade earlier.

The hospital — a story that stayed with him. Norman visited the site where, during the Japanese occupation in December 1941, forces took control of a hospital. The nurses were given a terrible choice: walk into the sea, or be shot. Many walked into the water. Standing there only ten years after it happened, Norman felt the full weight of it.

The typhoons. When the high winds came, the ship was moved from the docks and tied to a buoy in the harbour to ride out the storm. The lightning that accompanied the typhoons lit up the entire harbour — an awesome and frightening sight for a young sailor far from home.

The Return

The long way home

After Hong Kong, Norman retraced the entire outward route — Penang, Singapore, Bombay, Colombo, Aden, the Red Sea, the Suez Canal, Port Said — and back out into the Mediterranean and home to London. The return journey took roughly the same time as the outward voyage. The same ports, but seen now through eyes that had witnessed far more than when the ship first passed through.

Hong Kong → Penang → Singapore → Bombay → Colombo → Aden → Red Sea → Suez Canal → Port Said → London

The Records

Official documents

The Discharge Book

Every Merchant Navy sailor carried a Discharge Book — an official government record issued by the Labour Exchange. Each ship's captain would make an entry on discharge, grading the sailor's conduct and ability.

VG (Very Good) was the mark of a reliable sailor. DR (Decline to Report) was a serious black mark that could make finding future berths very difficult.
